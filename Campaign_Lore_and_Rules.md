# Campaign Lore & Rules: The Divine Preserve (Faldoran)

## 📖 The Lore

**The Premise**
The pantheon grew bored. Endless aeons of answering prayers and managing cosmic balance lacked the thrill of chaotic entertainment. Their solution was to carve out a pocket dimension—a massive, artificial "Hunting Preserve."

**The Lie**
The deities reached out across the multiverse to mortals, promising them untold power, glory, and wealth if they willingly journeyed to this new realm.

**The Truth**
To "journey" to this realm, the mortals had to die. Everyone who arrived woke up gasping for air, remembering the exact moment of their random, often sudden, demise in their old world. They didn't arrive as demigods; they arrived as 1st-level mortals at the absolute bottom of the food chain.

**The Ecosystem**
The world makes no ecological sense. A frozen tundra might border a scorching desert perfectly. The deities stocked this world with the absolute apex predators and most terrifying aberrations from across the planes. It is a zoo where the cages are open, and the players are the latest batch of "prey."

## ⚙️ Campaign Rules & Mechanics (D&D 3.5)

### 1. The Camp Roster (Survival Mechanics)
The starting population represents the entirety of "safe" civilization. There are no other massive cities or friendly kingdoms to trade with.
*   **Mechanic:** The NPCs are vital resources. If the players lose their last Blacksmith or Healer, they can no longer easily access those services until someone else learns the trade.
*   **The Goblin Attack:** The recent goblin attack that claimed 10 lives is a wake-up call. The players must actively assign roles to the survivors (e.g., Guards, Scavengers, Builders, Farmers) to keep the camp alive.

### 2. The Lie of Power (Accelerated Growth)
The gods promised power, and in a twisted way, the potential is there. Because the environment is a pressure cooker designed for legendary monsters, the ambient magic and experience yield are incredibly high.
*   **Mechanic:** You might consider allowing players to use Gestalt Character rules, or grant them a free bonus feat at 1st level to give them a fighting chance against the "best of the best monsters." (Optional, up to the DM).
*   **XP Cap:** The "ambient magic" is potent, but mortal bodies can only absorb so much so fast without burning out. There is a hard cap of **100 XP** that can be earned passively per month. The DM can override this setting directly for specific individuals if they achieve something extraordinary.
*   **Loot Drops:** Magic items are incredibly rare. The players might occasionally find the gear of previous, failed "contestants," but most resources must be crafted or scavenged from the environment.

### 3. Artificial Ecology (DM Encounter Rule)
Because this is a godly hunting preserve, you do not need to adhere to normal environmental restrictions for D&D 3.5 monsters.
*   **Mechanic:** You can seamlessly blend encounters. The players could fight a mind flayer, only for a hungry T-Rex to crash through the trees halfway through the fight. Invisible divine barriers or sudden magical climate-shifts keep the biomes separated.

### 4. The Revolving Door of Death (Revival)
In a world built by bored gods, death is not always an escape. The gods find entertainment in watching the same souls struggle again and again, and the magic of the preserve can sometimes knit a shattered body back together—for a price. 
*   **Mechanic:** If a PC dies, their soul remains tethered to the Preserve. They can be brought back by finding rare divine boons hidden in the world, bargaining with mysterious extraplanar entities, or tapping into the corrupted residual magic of the land. 
*   **The Cost:** Coming back from the dead in the Preserve is a traumatic ordeal. Revival might result in temporary stat penalties, a new rolling "Flaw" from the Cause of Death table, or owing a dangerous favor to whatever unseen force pulled them back to life.

### 4. Time Slips and Population Dynamics
The flow of time in the Divine Preserve is unstable, particularly near areas of concentrated ancient magic or "dungeons." This has profound effects on the town's growth.
*   **Time Slips:** When players enter specific dungeons or complete major quests, they may experience a "time slip." What feels like days to the players could translate into months or years passing in the town.
*   **Family Trees and Relationships:** To survive, the villagers must build a future. Characters forge romantic relationships over time. A family tree will be maintained to track marriages, offspring, and the town's lineage.
*   **Birth Rate:** As time slips occur, established couples will naturally have children. These children grow rapidly (due to the ambient magic) and eventually inherit traits, flaws, and classes from their parents.
*   **Death Rate:** To maintain a balanced ecosystem (and keep the town management from becoming overwhelmingly complex), a natural death rate and event-driven mortality stringency will kick in once the population reaches a certain threshold (e.g., 50+ residents). Overpopulation draws larger predators and plagues.

## 🎲 Cause of Death Table (d20)

Since everybody died to get here, their death should leave a mark. Every PC and NPC has a "Cause of Death" from their old world.

*   **Mechanic:** Players can roll a d20 (or choose) to determine how they died. This grants them one minor thematic **Flaw** and one thematic **Bonus Trait/Feat**.

| d20 | Cause of Death | Thematic Flaw | Thematic Bonus |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |

| **2** | **Burned at the Stake** | -2 penalty on saves vs. fire effects. | Resistance to Fire 2 or +2 bonus on Intimidate checks. |
| **3** | **Poisoned (Assassination)** | -2 penalty on saves vs. ingested poisons. | +2 bonus on saves vs. all other poisons; +2 Spot. |
| **4** | **Crushed by Falling Rocks** | -2 penalty on Reflex saves vs. falling objects. | +2 bonus to AC against Bludgeoning attacks. |
| **5** | **Execution (Beheading)** | -2 penalty vs. critical hits (or fear of blades). | +2 bonus on Fortitude saves or +2 on Intimidate. |
| **6** | **Starvation/Exposure** | Must consume twice the normal daily rations to avoid fatigue. | +2 bonus on Survival checks; Endurance feat. |
| **7** | **Illness/Plague** | -2 penalty vs. mundane diseases. | +2 bonus vs. magical diseases; +2 Heal checks. |
| **8** | **Betrayal (Stabbed in the Back)** | -2 penalty on Sense Motive checks (too trusting). | +2 bonus to AC when flanked (paranoia). |

| **20** | **Unknown/Woke up Dead** | Roll twice on Flaw table; DM chooses worst. | Roll twice on Bonus table; DM chooses best. |
